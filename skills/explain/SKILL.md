# Explain

The goal is that the learner *understands* — that the explanation reads easily, straight through, and leaves them holding a working mental model of the subject, not a pile of detail to assemble later. A flawless explanation that doesn't land has failed. Left to its defaults an explanation drifts the wrong way — bottom-up, jargon-heavy, everything dumped at once — so lean against each of those.

Start where the learner is. Get a rough read on what they already know — from how they asked, or by asking when you genuinely can't tell — because the whole picture has to connect to something they already hold or it has nowhere to attach. Then open top-down: lead with what the subject is, what it's for, why it matters, and map its handful of parts, so every later detail has somewhere to hang. This is a habit, not a template — there's no required one-line opener; just genuinely start from the whole. Keep the explanation in plain, spoken language throughout — short sentences, everyday words, a term defined the moment it appears, pitched to the level you read off them — and anchor the unfamiliar to the familiar as you go, because understanding is built from the known toward the unknown; reach for an analogy only when it truly makes something click. A part named but tied to nothing they already grasp has been heard, not understood.

Draw when structure beats prose: a small, labeled ASCII sketch — a flow, a decision, a hierarchy, the relationships among parts — earns its place when the shape is hard to hold in words. Skip it for a plain fact, or when the picture would be decorative, loosely related, or more tangled than the words it replaces; a diagram that adds load instead of lifting it isn't worth drawing.

Then judge whether to gate. Handing the map over for the learner to pick earns its round-trip only when explaining everything now would be a wall — many parts, or parts that each open into their own subtree. If the whole thing is a comfortable single read, skip the gate and explain the parts in the same pass; gating a small subject behind "which part?" is pure friction. Either way, honor an explicit signal ("all of it" / "one at a time").

When you drill down, name what you could open up next, let the learner pick, explain it the same way, and recurse as deep as they pull.

Watch whether it landed. Understanding isn't fire-and-forget: read the signal — a confused follow-up, a restatement that's subtly off, a question that reveals the frame didn't take — and when it hasn't taken, try again *differently*, not the same words louder: another angle, a smaller example, or a switch of modality, a sketch where prose stalled. Read this from the learner rather than quizzing them; "got it?" check-ins are friction, not comprehension.

If the subject can be found in the repo, explain from there, not from memory.
